> [!tip] Are you SafeintheBack?
- It is a legal Requirement for everyone in ambulances to wear seatbelts unless providing treatment which cannot be delayed [@2015motorvehicleswearing].
- Learn more at the [SafeintheBack Campaign](https://aace.org.uk/safeintheback/)

# Evidence Base
- The [Associate of Ambulance Chief Executives](https://aace.org.uk/safeintheback/) started this campaign after finding:
>[!quote] AACE Late 2022 Survey Key Findings:
>- Many staff did not know that it was the driver’s ultimate responsibility to ensure that all staff and patients in the vehicle were wearing seatbelts / harnesses before setting off.
> - A high proportion of staff did not wear a seat belt during any part of their most recent journey in an ambulance.
> - A relatively low percentage of staff wore seat belts during their entire most recent journey in an ambulance.

- Over 80% of ambulance crew reported knowledge of safely transporting children, but only about 30% of children were transported using the correct method. This was said by crew to be due to parents lap being seen as more appropriate or the [[Pedi-mate]] taking too long to attach [@johnson2006childproviderrestraints;@oneil2014ambulancetransportnoncritical]
- Percentage of crew who never wore a seatbelt rose from 1% to 20% between the cab and the saloon of the vehicle. Critically unwell patients increased this to about 80% [@cash2019seatbeltuse]
- Organisations also had a part to play in this: with seatbelt policies increasing usage of seatbelts about threefold [@cash2019seatbeltuse]. There have also been examples of ambulance design affecting safety (see [risk assessment from GMB Union](https://www.gmb-southern.org.uk/secamb-seatbelt-risk-assessment); *example provided is historical and not reflective of current practices*)
- In contrast, there was reports of need for coercion to maintain patients wearing seatbelts, which does present ethical and health issues for patients [@haikio2023ambulancepersonneluse]
